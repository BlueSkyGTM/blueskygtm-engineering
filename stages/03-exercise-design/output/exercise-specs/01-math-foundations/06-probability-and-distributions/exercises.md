# Exercises — Probability and Distributions

## Exercises

1. **Compute** P(reply | personalized_subject) from the following GTM email campaign counts: 1,200 emails sent with personalized subject lines, 288 of those replied; 1,000 emails sent without personalization, 150 of those replied. Also compute P(reply | not personalized) and print both values to the terminal with clear labels. State in a printed comment which strategy has the higher conditional reply probability and by how much.

2. **Generate** 10,000 samples each from three distributions relevant to GTM work: a normal distribution modeling deal size (mean $45,000, std $12,000), a binomial distribution modeling email opens out of 50 sends (p = 0.22), and a Poisson distribution modeling inbound leads per day (lambda = 7.5). Print the mean, standard deviation, min, and max for each sample set. Verify the sample means are close to the theoretical parameters you specified.

3. **Implement** a binomial PMF function from first principles — compute C(n, k) and multiply by p^k * (1-p)^(n-k) using only `math.factorial` or a hand-written combination function. Call your PMF for every k in range 0 through 20 with n=20 and p=0.35. Then validate every result against `scipy.stats.binom.pmf` for the same parameters. Print a table of k, your PMF value, scipy's value, and the absolute difference. The max difference across all k values should be effectively zero — print that max so you can confirm.

4. **Apply** Bayes' theorem to a real enrichment scenario. You have a CSV export of 500 accounts from Apollo (columns: `industry`, `employee_count`, `is_customer`). Load the data and compute P(customer | industry == "SaaS" and employee_count < 200) using direct frequency counting. Then compute the same quantity using Bayes' theorem — P(customer) * P(SaaS and small | customer) / P(SaaS and small). Print both results and confirm they match. If you do not have a real export, generate a synthetic dataset of 500 rows with a known structure and document the generation logic in a comment.

5. **Build** a z-score drift detector for lead-generation metrics. Write a module that accepts two lists of floats: a baseline window (e.g., 30 days of daily inbound lead counts) and a current window (the most recent 7 days). Compute the z-score of the current window's mean against the baseline distribution. Flag any metric whose z-score exceeds 2.0 as drifting. Run the detector on at least three different metrics (inbound leads, email reply rate as a proportion, and demo bookings) using baseline and current data you define. Save the module to `handlers/drift_detector.py` and print a summary report showing each metric, its z-score, and a DRIFT / OK flag.

6. **Design** a beta-binomial conversion rate updater. Start with a prior of Beta(2, 18) representing a skeptical belief about a landing page's conversion rate. Observe two rounds of evidence: Round 1 produces 45 conversions out of 300 visitors, and Round 2 produces 80 conversions out of 500 visitors. After each round, compute the posterior parameters, the posterior mean, and a 95% credible interval using `scipy.stats.beta.ppf`. Print the prior, both posteriors, and whether the conversion rate has meaningfully shifted between rounds. Save a short markdown summary of findings to `outputs/skill-conversion-bayes.md`.
