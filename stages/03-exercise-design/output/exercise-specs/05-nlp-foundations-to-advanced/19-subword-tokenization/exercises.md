## Exercises

1. **Implement BPE merge operations from scratch on a small corpus.** Build a BPE trainer that takes a list of input words, initializes the vocabulary at the character level, and iteratively merges the most frequent adjacent token pair. After each merge round, print the current vocabulary, the pair selected, and its frequency. Run your trainer on a 15–20 sentence corpus of your choice and perform 10 merge rounds. Confirm that frequent multi-character sequences (e.g., `"ing"`, `"tion"`) appear in the vocabulary by the final round.

2. **Compare tokenization output across BPE, WordPiece, and Unigram on identical input text.** Using the `tokenizers` library (Hugging Face), train a BPE, WordPiece, and Unigram tokenizer on the same small corpus. Feed each tokenizer an identical set of five test sentences — including at least two words not present in the training corpus. Print each sentence alongside the token splits from all three tokenizers in a readable side-by-side table. Verify that all three produce different segmentations for at least one out-of-vocabulary word.

3. **Train a SentencePiece Unigram model on a raw byte corpus and inspect vocabulary scores.** Install `sentencepiece`, collect a raw text corpus of at least 1,000 lines (use a dataset like WikiText or scraped product descriptions), and train a Unigram model with a vocabulary size of 2,000. Write a script that loads the trained `.model` file, prints the top 50 tokens by score, prints the bottom 50 tokens by score, and reports what percentage of the vocabulary consists of single-character or single-byte units. Confirm that SentencePiece treats the input as raw bytes and does not rely on whitespace pre-splitting.
